Aircraft Value Estimator

Pick a model, enter the year and total time. The estimator applies a typical business-jet depreciation curve to the model's new price, then adjusts for hours versus expected utilization (~380 hr/yr). Output is a rough market range, not an appraisal.

What this includes: baseline depreciation from new price by age, adjustment for total hours vs typical utilization (~380 hr/yr), and a residual-value floor based on the model's current low market quote.

What this does NOT include — and what often matters more:
  • Engine time remaining to overhaul (can swing value $1–4M on heavy jets)
  • MX program enrollment — CAMP, JSSI, MSP, ESP, Corporate Care, etc.
  • APU time remaining
  • FANS 1/A, CPDLC, ADS-B Out, EVS/SVS avionics upgrades
  • Recent paint and interior refurbishment
  • Damage history and 8130-3 trail
  • Lease/finance encumbrances and Part 135 conformity

For real valuation, pull from Aircraft Bluebook, Vref, JetNet/AMSTAT, or commission an ASA-certified appraisal.

Notes & Methodology

What this is

An apples-to-apples cost and performance comparator for the J3T fleet survey set: 22 business jets across Dassault, Cessna, Gulfstream, and Bombardier. Two tools — a comparison table for browsing, and a trip calculator that estimates fuel, time, and total trip cost at four cruise altitudes per aircraft, accounting for winds aloft.

How costs are computed

Hourly cost is broken into variable (per flight hour) components:

  • Fuel = burn (gph) × fuel price ($/gal). Burn varies by altitude.
  • MX reserves = an hourly accrual toward airframe maintenance events, scheduled inspections, and parts replacements.
  • Engine / APU reserves = hourly accrual toward engine overhauls and APU events. On programs like MSP, ESP, or Corporate Care this becomes a fixed per-hour fee paid to the OEM.

Fixed annual costs (crew, hangar, insurance, training) are not included in the trip calculator since they don't vary with the trip — they amortize against your annual hours. Add roughly $1,500–$3,000 per hour depending on aircraft size and annual utilization if you want a true all-in number.

Trip math

Distance is great-circle (haversine) between the two ICAOs. Initial bearing is used to resolve the wind at each cruise altitude into a headwind/tailwind component:

headwind = wind_speed × cos(wind_from_direction − route_bearing)

Ground speed = TAS − headwind. Block time = (distance ÷ ground speed) + 0.3 hr taxi/climb/descent overhead. Fuel and reserves accrue against block time.

Nonstop feasibility

An aircraft is flagged nonstop when great-circle distance ≤ its published NBAA-IFR range. Real-world fuel planning trims this further for weather, weight, alternates, and reserves — treat the boolean as "in the ballpark," not as a dispatch decision.

Wind altitude bands

Winds are entered at the eight standard winds-aloft levels for jets: FL180, FL240, FL300, FL340, FL370, FL410, FL450, FL510. Each aircraft's altitude band uses the nearest entered layer.

Data sources & confidence

  • Performance specs — manufacturer type certificate data sheets, AFM/POH performance tables, and Aviation Week aircraft surveys. High confidence.
  • Fuel burn — published long-range and high-speed cruise figures. High confidence at typical-cruise altitude; less precise at extreme altitudes.
  • Hourly costs — B&CA Operations Planning Guide (annual) and Conklin & de Decker model reports. Medium confidence — figures shift year-over-year and vary materially by operator. Verify against your MX provider and fuel cards before quoting.
  • Older variants (Challenger 600, Falcon 2000DX, 900DX) have thinner public cost data than current-production aircraft. Numbers are conservative estimates.
